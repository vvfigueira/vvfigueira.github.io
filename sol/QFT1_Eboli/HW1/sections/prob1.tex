\probitem{}

Let an element of the Lorentz group be represented by, \begin{align}U\qty(\Lambda) = \exp\qty(\frac\im 2\omega_{\mu\nu}M^{\mu\nu})\end{align} this is indirectly defined through 
the defining representation of the Lorentz group, which sets up the composition law, and also the inverse, 
\begin{align}
    \tensor{\Lambda}{^\alpha_\beta}\qty(\omega)\rightarrow U\qty(\Lambda_1) U\qty(\Lambda_2) = U(\Lambda_1\Lambda_2),\ \ \ U^{-1}(\Lambda)= U\qty(\Lambda^{\textnormal T}) %\rightarrow -\im\dv{\tensor{\Lambda}{^\alpha_\beta}}{\omega_{\mu\nu}}\eval_{\omega=0} = -\frac\im 2 \qty(\tensor{g}{^\alpha^\mu}\tensor{g}{_\beta^\nu}-\tensor{g}{^\alpha^\nu}\tensor{g}{_\beta^\mu})
\end{align} 
the Lie algebra of a group measures the non-commutative aspect of different group transformations, hence, an way in which the Lie algebra will show up is 
when analyzing composition of transformations, the best choice of composition of transformations to look is doing a transformation 1, after doing a transformation 2 and then 
undoing the transformation 1, this composition measures by how much the transformation 1 affects the way the transformation 2 should act, in other words, measures the non-commutative 
aspect of both transformations,
\begin{align}
    U\qty(\Lambda_1)U\qty(\Lambda_2)U^{-1}\qty(\Lambda_1) &=U(\Lambda_1\Lambda_2 \Lambda_1^{\textnormal T})
\end{align}
now expand on $\Lambda_2=\mathbbm 1+\omega_2$,
\begin{align}
    U\qty(\Lambda_1)U\qty(\mathbbm 1+\omega_2)U^{-1}\qty(\Lambda_1) &=U(\Lambda_1 \qty(\mathbbm 1+\omega_2) \Lambda_1^{\textnormal T})\\
    U\qty(\Lambda_1)\qty(\mathbbm 1+\frac\im 2{\omega_2}_{\mu\nu}M^{\mu\nu})U^{-1}\qty(\Lambda_1) &=U(\mathbbm 1+\Lambda_1 \omega_2 \Lambda_1^{\textnormal T})\\
    \mathbbm 1+U\qty(\Lambda_1)\frac\im 2{\omega_2}_{\mu\nu}M^{\mu\nu}U^{-1}\qty(\Lambda_1) &=\mathbbm 1 +\frac\im 2\tensor{\qty(\Lambda_1 \omega_2 \Lambda_1^{\textnormal T})}{_\alpha_\beta}M^{\alpha\beta}\\
    {\omega_2}_{\mu\nu}U\qty(\Lambda_1)M^{\mu\nu}U^{-1}\qty(\Lambda_1) &=\tensor{{\Lambda_1}}{_\alpha^\mu}\tensor{{\omega_2}}{_\mu_\nu}\tensor{{\Lambda_1}}{_\beta^\nu}M^{\alpha\beta}\\
    U\qty(\Lambda_1)M^{\mu\nu}U^{-1}\qty(\Lambda_1) &=\tensor{{\Lambda_1}}{_\alpha^\mu}\tensor{{\Lambda_1}}{_\beta^\nu}M^{\alpha\beta}
\end{align}
in this last step we factored out $\omega_2$ by using that both sides are antisymmetric in $\mu\nu$. This equation already is useful by itself, as it shows how the generators themselves transforms under a group 
action, but, as we're interested into the algebra of the group, we still need to expand around $\Lambda_1$,
\begin{align}
    U\qty(\mathbbm 1+\omega_1)M^{\mu\nu}U^{-1}\qty(\mathbbm 1+\omega_1) &=\qty(\tensor{g}{_\alpha^\mu}+\tensor{{\omega_1}}{_\alpha^\mu})\qty(\tensor{g}{_\beta^\nu}+\tensor{{\omega_1}}{_\beta^\nu})M^{\alpha\beta}\\
    \qty(\mathbbm 1 +\frac\im 2{\omega_1}_{\alpha\beta}M^{\alpha\beta})M^{\mu\nu}\qty(\mathbbm 1 -\frac\im 2{\omega_1}_{\alpha\beta}M^{\alpha\beta})&=M^{\mu\nu}+M^{\mu\beta}\tensor{{\omega_1}}{_\beta^\nu}+M^{\alpha\nu}\tensor{{\omega_1}}{_\alpha^\mu}\\
    M^{\mu\nu}+\frac\im 2{\omega_1}_{\alpha\beta}M^{\alpha\beta}M^{\mu\nu} -\frac\im 2{\omega_1}_{\alpha\beta}M^{\mu\nu}M^{\alpha\beta}&=M^{\mu\nu}+M^{\mu\beta}\tensor{{\omega_1}}{_\beta^\nu}+M^{\alpha\nu}\tensor{{\omega_1}}{_\alpha^\mu}\\
    \frac\im 2{\omega_1}_{\alpha\beta}\comm{M^{\alpha\beta}}{M^{\mu\nu}}&={\omega_1}_{\alpha\beta}\qty(-\tensor{g}{^\alpha^\nu}M^{\mu\beta}+M^{\alpha\nu}g^{\beta\mu})
\end{align}
to factorize $\omega_1$ we need to ensure both sides are antisymmetric, this can be simply done by antisymmetrizing the right hand side,
\begin{align}
    \frac\im 2{\omega_1}_{\alpha\beta}\comm{M^{\alpha\beta}}{M^{\mu\nu}}&=\frac12{\omega_1}_{\alpha\beta}\qty(-\tensor{g}{^\alpha^\nu}M^{\mu\beta}+M^{\alpha\nu}g^{\beta\mu}+\tensor{g}{^\beta^\nu}M^{\mu\alpha}-M^{\beta\nu}g^{\alpha\mu})\\
    \im \comm{M^{\alpha\beta}}{M^{\mu\nu}}&=-\tensor{g}{^\alpha^\nu}M^{\mu\beta}+M^{\alpha\nu}g^{\beta\mu}+\tensor{g}{^\beta^\nu}M^{\mu\alpha}-M^{\beta\nu}g^{\alpha\mu}
\end{align}
The sign on the right hand side is dependent of metric convention, here the answer is for the $\mqty(-&+&+&+)$ metric, the opposite sign metric has as answer an additional sign in the right hand side.

\probitem{}

In the last item we obtained the Lie algebra $\mathfrak {so}^+\qty(1,3)$, or as we argued $\mathfrak {sl}\qty(2,\mathbb C)$. The usual way of constructing representations of Lie algebras is to do by the highest weight 
method, that is, we set up all the Casimirs of the algebra and choose a maximal set of mutually commuting algebra elements, then we construct a vector space in which these chosen operators are diagonal, and lastly 
with the remaining algebra elements construct ladder operators which acts on the vector space by lowering and increasing the eigenvalues of the chosen mutually commuting operators. As a remainder --- and as will be 
relevant --- the main example of this process which 
is physically relevant is the computation of the representations of the group $SO(3)\cong SU(2)/{\mathbb Z_2}$, which have the algebra $\mathfrak {so}\qty(3)\cong \mathfrak{su}\qty(2)$,
\begin{align}
    \comm{J^i}{J^j}&=\im\tensor{\epsilon}{^i^j_k}J^k,\ \ \ i,j,k =1,2,3\label{eq:su2algebra}
\end{align}
the number of Casimirs of an algebra is not a trivial thing to obtain, for special cases, as semi-simple algebras, the number of independent Casimirs is equal to the rank of the algebra. Luckily, there is a complete 
classification of all the simple Lie algebras and their ranks, the $\mathfrak{su}\qty(2)$ algebra is simple of rank one, then there is just one independent Casimir, $C = \delta_{ij}J^iJ^j$. 
Usually $J^3$ is chosen as part of the maximal set of mutually commuting algebra elements --- in this case the \textit{set} is just one element ---. Representations are then indexed by two numbers,
\begin{align}
    C\ket{j,m} = j\qty(j+1)\ket{k,m}, \ \ \ J^3\ket{j,m} = m\ket{j,m}, \ \ \ j\in \frac12 \mathbb N,\ \ \ m = -j, -j+1,\cdots , j-1, j
\end{align}

The same process will happen for the Lorentz algebra, but now the rank of the algebra is two, it can be verified that the two Casimirs are, 
\begin{align}
    C_1 = \frac12 M^{\mu\nu}M_{\mu\nu},\ \ \ C_2 = \frac14 \epsilon_{\mu\nu\alpha\beta}M^{\mu\nu}M^{\alpha\beta}
\end{align}
sadly, is not that enlightening to workout the representations in a covariant manner, it's easier to decompose $M^{\mu\nu}$ into it's spatial parts,
\begin{align}
    J_i &=\frac12\epsilon_{ijk}M^{jk},\ \ \ K^i= M^{i0}
\end{align}
now we have to compute the algebra in terms of these new generators, first the $J-J$ commutation,
\begin{align}
    \comm{J_i}{J_j}&=\frac14\epsilon_{iab}\epsilon_{jlm}\comm{M^{ab}}{M^{lm}}\\
    &=\frac\im 4\epsilon_{iab}\epsilon_{jlm}\qty(g^{al}M^{bm}-g^{bl}M^{am}+g^{bm}M^{al}-g^{am}M^{bl})\\
    &=\frac\im 2\epsilon_{iab}\epsilon_{jlm}\qty(g^{al}M^{bm}+g^{bm}M^{al})\\
    &=\frac\im 2\epsilon_{iab}\epsilon_{jlm}\qty(g^{al}M^{bm}-g^{am}M^{bl})\\
    &=\im\epsilon_{iab}\epsilon_{jlm}g^{al}M^{bm}=\im\tensor{\epsilon}{_i^l_b}\epsilon_{jlm}M^{bm}\\
    &=\im\tensor{\epsilon}{^l_b_i}\epsilon_{lmj}M^{bm}=\im\qty(g_{bm}g_{ij}-\tensor{g}{_b_j}\tensor{g}{_i_m})M^{bm},\ \ \ \textnormal{antisymmetrize }(b\leftrightarrow m)\\
    &=-\frac\im2\qty(\tensor{g}{_b_j}\tensor{g}{_m_i}-\tensor{g}{_b_i}\tensor{g}{_m_j})M^{bm}=\frac\im2\tensor{\epsilon}{_l_b_m}\tensor{\epsilon}{^l_i_j}M^{bm}\\
    &=\im\tensor{\epsilon}{_i_j^l}J_l
\end{align}
now the $J-K$,
\begin{align}
    \comm{J_i}{K^j}&=\frac12\epsilon_{iab}\comm{M^{ab}}{M^{j0}}\\
    &=\frac\im2\epsilon_{iab}\qty(\tensor{g}{^a^j}M^{b0}-\tensor{g}{^b^j}M^{a0}+g^{b0}\tensor{M}{^a^j}-g^{a0}\tensor{M}{^b^j})\\
    &=\frac\im2\epsilon_{iab}2\tensor{g}{^a^j}M^{b0}=\im\tensor{\epsilon}{_i^j_b}M^{b0}\\
    &=\im\tensor{\epsilon}{_i^j_k}K^k
\end{align}
lastly $K-K$,
\begin{align}
    \comm{K^i}{K^j}&=\comm{M^{i0}}{M^{j0}}\\
    &=\im\qty(g^{ij}M^{00}-\tensor{g}{^i^0}\tensor{M}{^0^j}+\tensor{g}{^0^0}M^{ij}-\tensor{g}{^0^j}\tensor{M}{^i_0})\\
    &=-\im M^{ij}=-\im \tensor{g}{^i_a}\tensor{g}{^j_b}M^{ab},\ \ \ \textnormal{antisymmetrize }(a\leftrightarrow b)\\
    &=-\frac\im2\qty(\tensor{g}{^i_a}\tensor{g}{^j_b}-\tensor{g}{^i_b}\tensor{g}{^j_a})M^{ab}\\
    &=-\frac\im2\tensor{\epsilon}{^l^i^j}\epsilon_{lab}M^{ab}\\
    &=-\im\epsilon^{ijk}J_{k}
\end{align}

Just for clarity sake, let's rewrite this new representation of the full $\mathfrak {sl}\qty(2,\mathbb C)$ algebra,
\begin{align} 
    \comm{J_i}{J_j}&=\im\tensor{\epsilon}{_i_j^k}J_k\\
    \comm{J_i}{K^j}&=\im\tensor{\epsilon}{_i^j_k}K^k\\
    \comm{K^i}{K^j}&=-\im\epsilon^{ijk}J_{k}
\end{align}
we just have been careful with the index placement, but, for our choice of metric $g_{ij}=\delta_{ij}$, thus, $J^i = J_i, K^i = K_i$.

The usual treatment is to define now two new combination of generators,
\begin{align}
    N_i&=\frac12\qty(J_i-\im K_i),\ \ \ N^\dagger_i=\frac12\qty(J_i+\im K_i)\label{eq:noperators}
\end{align}
this is not entirely justifiable, at the most fundamental level a Lie algebra is a vector space over the \textbf{real} numbers, a multiplication by some imaginary quantity is 
not well defined, as it even does not preserve the structure of the Lie bracket. The working procedure is to consider a complexification of a real Lie algebra, 
$\mathfrak g\hookrightarrow \mathfrak g_{\mathbb C}$ where the complexified Lie algebra $\mathfrak g_{\mathbb C}$ is understood as a real Lie algebra. This is 
done by considering the generators $T^I\in\mathfrak g$ linearly independent from $\im T^I$. Let's illustrate this process by working what is the complexification of 
the $\mathfrak {su}(2)$ algebra, we start from the algebra, \cref{eq:su2algebra}, and define the complexified algebra $\mathfrak {su}(2)_{\mathbb C}$ to be generated by 
$J^i, \im J^i$, to stress that we are considering them to be linearly independent, we are going to rename $\im J^i$ to $K^i$. Now we compute the algebra from \cref{eq:su2algebra} 
with these new set of generators, first between $J^i$ and $\im J^j = K^j$
\begin{align}
    \comm{J^i}{K^j}&=\comm{J^i}{\im J^j} = \im\comm{J^i}{J^j} =\im^2\tensor{\epsilon}{^i^j_k}J ^k = \im\tensor{\epsilon}{^i^j_k}\im J ^k= \im\tensor{\epsilon}{^i^j_k}K ^k 
\end{align}
now between $\im J^i = K^i$ and $\im J^j = K^j$,
\begin{align}
    \comm{K^i}{K^j}&=\comm{\im J^i}{\im J^j} = \im^2\comm{J^i}{J^j} =\im^2\im\tensor{\epsilon}{^i^j_k}J ^k = -\im\tensor{\epsilon}{^i^j_k} J ^k
\end{align}
for clarity, the algebra we found for $\mathfrak {su}\qty(2)_{\mathbb C}$ in the basis $J^i, \im J^i= K^i$ is,
\begin{align} 
    \comm{J^i}{J^j}&=\im\tensor{\epsilon}{^i^j_k}J^k\\
    \comm{J^i}{K^j}&=\im\tensor{\epsilon}{^i^j_k}K^k\\
    \comm{K^i}{K^j}&=-\im\tensor{\epsilon}{^i^j_k}J_{k}
\end{align}
exactly the $\mathfrak{sl}\qty(2,\mathbb C)$ algebra! In other words $\mathfrak {su}\qty(2)_{\mathbb C}\cong \mathfrak{sl}\qty(2,\mathbb C)$.

After this detour, we can go back to introducing \cref{eq:noperators}, which now we're aware that implies we're complexifying $\mathfrak{sl}\qty(2,\mathbb C)$, the new basis of 
generators in the complexified algebra $\mathfrak {sl}\qty(2,\mathbb C)_{\mathbb C}$ are $J^i,K^i,\im J^i,\im K^i$, which can be exchanged by $N_ i, \im N_ i, N^\dagger_i, \im N^\dagger_i$. 
It suffices to work out the $N-N^\dagger$ algebra, the others will follow, first $N-N$,
\begin{align}
    \comm{N_i}{N_j}&=\frac14\comm{J_i-\im K_i}{J_j-\im K_j}\\
    &=\frac\im4\epsilon_{ijk}J^k+\frac14\epsilon_{ijk}K^k+\frac14\epsilon_{ijk}K^k+\frac\im4\epsilon_{ijk}J^k\\
    &=\im\epsilon_{ijk}\frac12\qty(J^k-\im K^k)\\
    &=\im\epsilon_{ijk}N^k
\end{align}
then $N^\dagger-N^\dagger$,
\begin{align}
    \comm{N^\dagger_i}{N^\dagger_j}&=\frac14\comm{J_i+\im K_i}{J_j+\im K_j}\\
    &=\frac\im4\epsilon_{ijk}J^k-\frac14\epsilon_{ijk}K^k-\frac14\epsilon_{ijk}K^k+\frac\im4\epsilon_{ijk}J^k\\
    &=\im\epsilon_{ijk}\frac12\qty(J^k+\im K^k)\\
    &=\im\epsilon_{ijk}{N^\dagger}^k
\end{align}
and lastly $N-N^\dagger$,
\begin{align}
    \comm{N_i}{N^\dagger_j}&=\frac14\comm{J_i-\im K_i}{J_j+\im K_j}\\
    &=\frac\im4\epsilon_{ijk}j^k-\frac14\epsilon_{ijk}K^k+\frac14\epsilon_{ijk}K^k-\frac\im4\epsilon_{ijk}J^k\\
    &=0
\end{align}
together with the remaining commutations of $\im N, \im N^\dagger$. Thus, to summarize, the algebra of $\mathfrak {sl}\qty(2,\mathbb C)_{\mathbb C}$ is,
\begin{align}
    \comm{N_i}{N_j}&=\im\tensor{\epsilon}{_i_j^k}N_k\\
    \comm{N^\dagger_i}{N^\dagger_j}&=\im\tensor{\epsilon}{_i_j^k}N^\dagger_k,\ \ \ \ \textnormal{Plus commutators involving }\im N, \im N^\dagger\\
    \comm{N_i}{N^\dagger_j}&=0
\end{align}
but notice, the algebra generated by $N$ alone is $\mathfrak {su}(2)$, as is the one generated by $N^\dagger $ alone, by the commutation 
relations the algebra from $N$ is decoupled from the algebra of $N^\dagger$, thus, to go from the algebra generated by $N$ to the algebra generated by $N,\im N$ is simply the 
complexification of the algebra of $N$. Hence, the algebra generated by $N,\im N$ is $\mathfrak {su}\qty(2)_{\mathbb C}\cong \mathfrak {sl}\qty(2,\mathbb C)$, the same is valid for 
the pair $N^\dagger, \im N^\dagger$. As the algebras between $N$ and $N^\dagger$ are independent, so are the algebras between $N,\im N$ and $N^\dagger, \im N^\dagger$, what implies that 
$\mathfrak{sl}\qty(2,\mathbb C)_{\mathbb C}$, which is the algebra generated by $N,\im N,N^\dagger, \im N^\dagger$ is just the sum of the two independent algebras, in other words, 
\begin{align}
    \mathfrak{sl}\qty(2,\mathbb C)_{\mathbb C}&\cong \mathfrak {su}\qty(2)_{\mathbb C}\oplus \mathfrak {su}\qty(2)_{\mathbb C}\\
    \mathfrak{sl}\qty(2,\mathbb C)_{\mathbb C}&\cong \mathfrak{sl}\qty(2,\mathbb C)\oplus\mathfrak{sl}\qty(2,\mathbb C)
\end{align}

The first form of this result is of the most significance for us,
\begin{align}
    \mathfrak{sl}\qty(2,\mathbb C)_{\mathbb C}&\cong \mathfrak {su}\qty(2)_{\mathbb C}\oplus \mathfrak {su}\qty(2)_{\mathbb C}\cong \qty(\mathfrak {su}\qty(2)\oplus \mathfrak {su}\qty(2))_{\mathbb C}
\end{align}
Thus, the real representations of $\mathfrak{sl}\qty(2,\mathbb C)_{\mathbb C}$ are into a one-to-one map with the real representations of $\qty(\mathfrak {su}\qty(2)\oplus \mathfrak {su}\qty(2))_{\mathbb C}$, 
what is equivalent to say that the representations of $\mathfrak{sl}\qty(2,\mathbb C)$ are into a one-to-one with representations of $\mathfrak {su}\qty(2)\oplus \mathfrak {su}\qty(2)$. 
Of course, these two $\mathfrak {su}(2)$ are the ones generated by $N, N^\dagger$. 

This whole convoluted argument is just to justify why is ok --- in this case --- to multiply the generators by complex numbers and still obtain a reasonable answer. To finish to find the 
representation of the Lorentz algebra, we continue as always in the usual procedure of finding the representations of $\mathfrak{su}(2)$ which we already described, there are two 
Casimirs, 
\begin{align}
    C_L=\delta^{ij}N_i N_j,\ \ \  C_R = \delta^{ij}N^\dagger_i N^\dagger_j
\end{align}
choosing also as a mutually commuting set of algebra elements $N_3,N^\dagger_3$, then the representations are given by four numbers,
\begin{align}
    C_L\ket{j_L,m_L;j_R,m_R} &= j_L\qty(j_L+1)\ket{j_L,m_L;j_R,m_R} ,\ C_R\ket{j_L,m_L;j_R,m_R} = j_R\qty(j_R+1)\ket{j_L,m_L;j_R,m_R}\\
    N_3\ket{j_L,m_L;j_R,m_R} &= m_L \ket{j_L,m_L;j_R,m_R}, \ N^\dagger_3\ket{j_L,m_L;j_R,m_R} = m_R \ket{j_L,m_L;j_R,m_R}\\
    j_{L/R} &\in\frac12\mathbb N,\ \ \ m_{L/R} = -j_{L/R},-j_{L/R}+1,\cdots,j_{L/R}-1,j_{L/R}
\end{align}
the representations are indexed by $j_{L/R}$, generally are written as,
\begin{align}
    \qty(j_L,j_R)
\end{align}

The simplest possible representation is the scalar one, $j_L=j_R=0$, this fixes,
\begin{align}
    \qty(0,0)\rightarrow N_ i= N_i^\dagger =0 \Rightarrow J^i=K^i =0\Rightarrow M^{\mu\nu}=0\Rightarrow U\qty(\Lambda) = 1
\end{align}
$U\qty(\Lambda)=1$ is what we should expect from a scalar representation, as a scalar do not transform under Lorentz. Now, the simplest non trivial 
representation we can obtain is given by either $j_L=\frac12, j_R=0$ or $j_R=\frac12, j_L=0$ they are called the Left-handed Weyl spinorial representation 
and the Right-handed Weyl spinorial representation respectively. As example let's pickup the Left-handed one, we know that the $j=\frac12$ representation of 
$\mathfrak {su}(2)$ is given by the Pauli matrices, then,
\begin{align}
    \qty(\frac12,0)\rightarrow N_ i=\frac12\sigma_i,\  N_i^\dagger =0 \Rightarrow J^i=\frac12 \sigma^i,\ K^i =\frac\im 2\sigma^i\Rightarrow M^{ij}=\frac12 \tensor{\epsilon}{^i^j_k}\sigma^k,\ M^{i0}=\frac\im2\sigma^i
\end{align}
the Right-handed one has a similar form. Now, a interesting representation is the vector one,
\begin{align}
    \qty(\frac12,\frac12)=\qty(\frac12,0)\otimes\qty(0,\frac12)
\end{align}
to see why this should be a vector representation we need to remember some results about sum of angular momentum, when going from the $N^2, N^{\dagger2}$ basis to the 
$\qty(N+N^\dagger)^2$ basis, we exchange the $j_{L/R}$ for a total $j=\abs{j_L -j_R},\cdots, j_L+j_R$. Notice that $j(j+1)$ is the eigenvalue of $(N+N^\dagger)^2$, but, 
\begin{align}
    (N+N^\dagger)^2&=J^2
\end{align}
hence the $j$ is exactly the angular momentum of the representation. For what we called the vector representation,
\begin{align}
    \qty(\frac12,\frac12)=\vb 0\oplus \vb 1
\end{align}
it carries only a scalar, under rotations, and a vector, under rotations, hence the name vector representation.
