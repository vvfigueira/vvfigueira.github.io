\problem{}

The first two itens are just some well known properties of Lorentz transformations, the last item is other 
important application of Noether's theorem, which gives the angular momentum density tensor, an analogous 
quantity to the energy-momentum tensor, but for Lorentz transformations instead of translations.

\probitem{}

A Lorentz transformation has the following infinitesimal form when acting on a vector,
\begin{align}
    {x'}^\mu &= \tensor{\Lambda}{^\mu_\nu}x^\nu=x^\mu+\tensor{\omega}{^\mu_\nu}x^\nu+\mathcal O\qty(\omega^2)
\end{align}
it's defining property is to preserve the scalar product between any vectors,
\begin{align}
    y^\mu x_\mu &= {y'}^\mu{x'}_\mu\\
    y^\mu x_\mu &= \qty(y^\mu+\tensor{\omega}{^\mu_\nu}y^\nu+\mathcal O\qty(\omega^2))\qty(x_\mu+\tensor{\omega}{_\mu_\alpha}x^\alpha+\mathcal O\qty(\omega^2))\\
    y^\mu x_\mu &=  y^\mu x_\mu +\tensor{\omega}{^\mu_\nu}y^\nu x_\mu + y^\mu\tensor{\omega}{_\mu_\alpha}x^\alpha+\mathcal O\qty(\omega^2)\\
    0 &=  \tensor{\omega}{^\mu_\nu}y^\nu x_\mu + y^\mu\tensor{\omega}{_\mu_\alpha}x^\alpha+\mathcal O\qty(\omega^2)
\end{align}
as $\omega$ is arbitrary, the only possible way the right-hand side is zero is if each coefficient of each order in $\omega$ is zero,
at first order,
\begin{align}
    0&=\tensor{\omega}{_\mu_\nu}y^\nu x^\mu + \tensor{\omega}{_\mu_\nu}x^\nu y^\mu\\
    0&=y^\nu x^\mu\qty(\tensor{\omega}{_\mu_\nu} + \tensor{\omega}{_\nu_\mu})\Rightarrow \tensor{\omega}{_\mu_\nu} + \tensor{\omega}{_\nu_\mu}=0\Rightarrow \tensor{\omega}{_\mu_\nu} =- \tensor{\omega}{_\nu_\mu}
\end{align}
the lasts equalities follow from the equation being needed to hold for arbitrary values of $x,y$. We conclude that $\omega$ is anti-symmetric.

\probitem{}

A rotation should preserve the time component,
\begin{align}
    x^0={x'}^0&=x^0+\tensor{\omega}{^0_\nu}x^\nu\\
    0&=\tensor{\omega}{^0_\nu}x^\nu\Rightarrow \tensor{\omega}{^0_\nu}=0\Rightarrow \tensor{\omega}{_0_\nu}=0
\end{align}
together with the anti-symmetry gives, 
\begin{align}
    \tensor{\omega}{_\mu_\nu} = \mqty(0&0&0&0\\0&0&\omega_{12}&-\omega_{31}\\0&-\omega_{12}&0&\omega_{23}\\0&\omega_{31}&-\omega_{23}&0)
\end{align}
now, to understand better what each of these components are we can look at a general representation of a spatial rotation. Let $\boldsymbol \theta$ be 
the rotation direction and $\theta$ be it's modulus,
\begin{align}
    \vb x '=\vb x\cos \theta+\boldsymbol \theta\times \vb x\sin\theta + \qty(\boldsymbol \theta\cdot \vb x)\boldsymbol \theta\qty(1-\cos\theta)
\end{align}
to first order we have,
\begin{align}
    {x'}^i=\tensor{\epsilon}{^i_j_k}\boldsymbol \theta^j x^k \theta=\tensor{\omega}{^i_k}x^k\Rightarrow \tensor{\omega}{_i_k} = -\tensor{\epsilon}{_k_i_j}\theta\boldsymbol \theta^j
\end{align}
we recognize from this last expression that $\theta\boldsymbol \theta^j$ is just the infinitesimal angle rotation in the $k-i$ plane, hence, we call it $\theta^j$. Also, the 
equivalence between the two formulation of the rotations implies that $\omega_{ik}$ is minus the angle of rotation in the $k-i$ plane. With this we can rewrite our matrix as,
\begin{align}
    \tensor{\omega}{_\mu_\nu} = \mqty(0&0&0&0\\0&0&-\theta^3&\theta^2\\0&\theta^3&0&-\theta^1\\0&-\theta^2&\theta^1&0)
\end{align}

\probitem{}

The first step to derive the Noether current is to state the Lorentz transformation for a scalar field,
\begin{align}
    \begin{cases}
        x^\mu&\rightarrow {x'}^\mu=\tensor{\Lambda}{^\mu_\nu}\qty(\omega)x^\nu\\
        \phi\qty(x)&\rightarrow {\phi'}\qty(x')=\phi\qty(x)
    \end{cases}
\end{align}
which is parametrized by $\omega_{\mu\nu}$, now we should take the variation of the coordinates,
\begin{align}
    \dv{{x'}^\mu}{\omega^{\alpha\beta}}\eval_{\omega_{\alpha\beta}=0}=\dv{}{\omega_{\alpha\beta}}\qty(x^\mu+\tensor{\omega}{^\mu_\nu}x^\nu+\mathcal O\qty(\omega^2))\eval_{\omega_{\alpha\beta}=0}
\end{align}
may look trivial that $\dv{\omega_{\mu\nu}}{\omega_{\alpha\beta}}\stackrel{?}{=}\tensor{g}{_\mu^\alpha}\tensor{g}{_\nu^\beta}$, but this is wrong, as $\omega$ 
is anti-symmetric, it's true that $1=\dv{\omega_{12}}{\omega_{12}}=-\dv{\omega_{21}}{\omega_{12}}$, the last expression doesn't takes into consideration 
this, it's necessary to antisymmetrize it, hence,
\begin{align}
    \dv{{x'}^\mu}{\omega^{\alpha\beta}}\eval_{\omega_{\alpha\beta}=0}=\qty(\tensor{g}{_\alpha^\mu}\tensor{g}{_\beta_\nu}-\tensor{g}{_\beta^\mu}\tensor{g}{_\alpha_\nu})x^\nu=\tensor{g}{_\alpha^\mu}x_\beta-\tensor{g}{_\beta^\mu}x_\alpha\label{eq:xderivativelorentz}
\end{align}
we also need the expression of the field variation, this is obtained by usual means of writing the transformation law as $\phi'(x)=\phi(\Lambda^{\textnormal T}x)$,
\begin{align}
    \dv{\phi'\qty(x)}{\omega^{\alpha\beta}}\eval_{\omega^{\alpha\beta}=0}&=\dv{}{\omega^{\alpha\beta}}\qty(\phi\qty(\Lambda^{\textnormal T}x))\eval_{\omega^{\alpha\beta}=0}=\partial_\nu\phi\qty(x)\dv{}{\omega^{\alpha\beta}}\qty(\Lambda^{\textnormal T}x)^\nu\eval_{\omega^{\alpha\beta}=0}\\
    \dv{\phi'\qty(x)}{\omega^{\alpha\beta}}\eval_{\omega^{\alpha\beta}=0}&=\partial_\nu\phi\qty(x)\dv{\tensor{\Lambda}{_\rho^\nu}}{\omega^{\alpha\beta}}\eval_{\omega^{\alpha\beta}=0}x^\rho=\partial_\nu\phi\qty(x)\qty(\tensor{g}{_\alpha_\rho}\tensor{g}{_\beta^\nu}-\tensor{g}{_\beta_\rho}\tensor{g}{_\alpha^\nu})x^\rho\\
    \dv{\phi'\qty(x)}{\omega^{\alpha\beta}}\eval_{\omega^{\alpha\beta}=0}&=x_\alpha\partial_\beta\phi-x_\beta\partial_\alpha\phi
\end{align}
again we use these variation expressions on \cref{eq:conservedcurrentnoether},
\begin{align}
    \tensor{{\tilde M}}{^\mu_\alpha_\beta} &=\pdv{\mathcal L}{\partial_\mu\phi\qty(x)}\dv{\phi'\qty(x)}{\omega^{\alpha\beta}}\eval_{\omega^{\alpha\beta}=0}+\mathcal L\dv{{x'}^\mu}{\omega^{\alpha\beta}}\eval_{\omega^{\alpha\beta}=0}\\
    \tensor{{\tilde M}}{^\mu_\alpha_\beta} &=\pdv{\mathcal L}{\partial_\mu\phi\qty(x)}\qty(x_\alpha\partial_\beta\phi-x_\beta\partial_\alpha\phi)+\mathcal L\qty(\tensor{g}{_\alpha^\mu}x_\beta-\tensor{g}{_\beta^\mu}x_\alpha)\\
    \tensor{{\tilde M}}{^\mu_\alpha_\beta} &=-x_\alpha\qty(-\pdv{\mathcal L}{\partial_\mu\phi\qty(x)}\partial_\beta\phi+\mathcal L\tensor{g}{_\beta^\mu})+x_\beta\qty(-\pdv{\mathcal L}{\partial_\mu\phi\qty(x)}\partial_\alpha\phi+\mathcal L\tensor{g}{_\alpha^\mu})\\
    \tensor{{\tilde M}}{^\mu_\alpha_\beta} &=x_\beta \tensor{T}{^\mu_\alpha}-x_\alpha \tensor{T}{^\mu_\beta}
\end{align}
where in the last line we identified the energy-momentum tensor. The conserved current is defined up to a constant, then we can change 
the sign of the quantity to our desire. It's more common to work with the opposite sign conserved current $M =-{\tilde M} $,
\begin{align}
    \tensor{M}{^\mu_\alpha_\beta} &=x_\alpha \tensor{T}{^\mu_\beta}-x_\beta \tensor{T}{^\mu_\alpha}
\end{align}
this sign choice is related to the conserved charge associated with $M$. Remember, the conserved charge given by the integral 
of the energy-momentum tensor is the four momentum,
\begin{align}
    P_\beta = \int\dd[3]{\vb x}\tensor{T}{^0_\beta}
\end{align}
thus, $\tensor{T}{^0_\beta}$ is the density of four momentum. With this information, the conserved quantity,
\begin{align}
    M_{\alpha\beta} = \int\dd[3]{\vb x}\tensor{M}{^0_\alpha_\beta}=\int\dd[3]{\vb x}\qty(x_\alpha \tensor{T}{^0_\beta}-x_\beta \tensor{T}{^0_\alpha})
\end{align}
is the integral of position times density of momentum, anti-symmetrized. It's clear the resemblance to: $\vb L = \vb x\times \vb p$. We can 
even verify that $M_{\alpha\beta}$ as given by the above expression is a representation of the Lorentz algebra generators, but this 
representation lives in the field phase-space. That is, the algebra is obtained by the Poisson bracket,
\begin{align}
    \acomm{M^{\alpha\beta}}{M^{\mu\nu}}&=\tensor{g}{^\alpha^\nu}M^{\mu\beta}-M^{\alpha\nu}g^{\beta\mu}-\tensor{g}{^\beta^\nu}M^{\mu\alpha}+M^{\beta\nu}g^{\alpha\mu}
\end{align}
of course, without the $\im$. This is so due to usually in classical mechanics we use the real version of the generators, as complex numbers are 
not so welcome.

Without a proper lagrangian, or other information about the theory, we cannot explicitly check that it's conserved. But, the 
condition of conservation of this current imposes other constrains in the theory,
\begin{align}
    \partial_\mu \tensor{M}{^\mu_\alpha_\beta}=0&= \partial_\mu\qty(x_\alpha \tensor{T}{^\mu_\beta}-x_\beta \tensor{T}{^\mu_\alpha})\\
    0&= \partial_\mu x_\alpha \tensor{T}{^\mu_\beta}+x_\alpha\partial_\mu \tensor{T}{^\mu_\beta}-\partial_\mu x_\beta \tensor{T}{^\mu_\alpha}- x_\beta\partial_\mu \tensor{T}{^\mu_\alpha}\\
    0&= g_{\mu\alpha} \tensor{T}{^\mu_\beta}+x_\alpha\partial_\mu \tensor{T}{^\mu_\beta}-g_{\mu\beta} \tensor{T}{^\mu_\alpha}- x_\beta\partial_\mu \tensor{T}{^\mu_\alpha}\\
    0&= \tensor{T}{_\alpha_\beta}-\tensor{T}{_\beta_\alpha}+x_\alpha\partial_\mu \tensor{T}{^\mu_\beta}- x_\beta\partial_\mu \tensor{T}{^\mu_\alpha}
\end{align}
notice, if we have a Lorentz invariant scalar theory, which is also translational invariant, then, 
\begin{align}
    0&= \tensor{T}{_\alpha_\beta}-\tensor{T}{_\beta_\alpha}
\end{align}
the energy-momentum tensor is symmetric!
